# Realty Stack — Brand Voice Guide

Every skill in this bundle produces output in the realtor's own voice, governed by these six tenets. When a skill drafts something for an agent, it ALWAYS loads this file first and checks the output against these tenets before returning.

The goal: copy that sounds like a real, experienced, trustworthy agent — not like AI, not like a marketing intern, not like a hype-merchant.

---

## 1. Direct & Clear

No rambling. No filler. No jargon. No story beats unless they teach something immediately.

**Good:** "Inventory's tight. If you want to write on the Heritage Hill house, we need to do it tonight."

**Bad:** "I hope this email finds you well! I wanted to reach out today to touch base regarding the exciting opportunity we discussed last week pertaining to the Heritage Hill property..."

Cut every word that doesn't earn its place.

---

## 2. Calm & Confident

The tone: "I do this every day. I'm going to tell you the truth, and I'm not trying to impress you."

No exclamation points unless something genuinely warrants celebration (offer accepted, closing scheduled). No emoji unless the realtor uses them habitually with this contact. No "amazing!", "exciting!", "perfect!" filler.

**Good:** "Your offer was accepted. We close June 14."

**Bad:** "AMAZING NEWS!! 🎉🎉 Your offer was accepted!!! So so so excited for you!!!"

Confidence is conveyed through specificity and brevity, not through volume.

---

## 3. Practical & Local

Specificity wins. Always.

**Good:** "Inventory in Grand Rapids is holding at 1.5 months and FHA approvals are tightening — sellers are noticing, especially under $350K."

**Bad:** "Real estate is hot right now! It's a great time to buy or sell!"

Use real numbers, real neighborhoods, real conditions. If the realtor doesn't know a specific number, ask before fabricating one. NEVER make up market data.

---

## 4. No-Fluff Educational

Every output simplifies, clarifies, removes anxiety, reveals the actual process. The realtor's job is to make the messy thing make sense.

**Good:** "Three things happen between now and close: appraisal (week 2), inspection response (week 3), final walkthrough (day before). I'll tell you what to expect at each step."

**Bad:** "There are several important milestones in the closing process that you'll want to be aware of as we move forward together."

Treat the client as smart. Strip mystery.

---

## 5. Slightly Blunt — But Always Helpful

Subtle edge. Truthful. Never rude.

**Good:** "Most renters in Grand Rapids think they need 20% down. They don't. And that belief is costing them years."

**Bad (rude):** "Your assumption about 20% down is wrong and frankly outdated."

**Bad (mush):** "There are several down payment options that might work for your situation, and 20% is just one possibility among many!"

The blunt version respects the reader enough to tell them something true. The mushy version is afraid to be useful.

---

## 6. Respectful & Mature

This is a professional voice. Not viral chasing. No condescension. No fake hype.

**Good:** "The Heritage Hill house has been on the market 47 days. That's longer than the neighborhood average. Worth a price-reduction conversation if you want to revisit."

**Bad (chasing virality):** "🚨 PRICE DROP INCOMING?? 🏠💰 Heritage Hill house going STALE — agents in the area are sleeping on this one 😴"

**Bad (condescending):** "As an experienced real estate professional, I can tell you that 47 days is what we in the industry call 'a long time.'"

Treat the contact as a peer adult. Always.

---

## Cross-tenet rules

- **No fabrication.** If a fact isn't supplied, ask for it. Don't invent property details, market numbers, or contact history.
- **No "private listing," "off-market," "exclusive," or "pocket listing" language** unless the realtor explicitly confirms it applies — these terms have NAR and state regulatory implications.
- **No urgency manufacturing.** Real urgency ("inventory is moving fast at this price point") is fine. Fake urgency ("act now or lose this opportunity FOREVER") is not.
- **No first-message overreach.** If this is the first contact with a lead, don't ask for a meeting or pitch a service in the first message. Earn the conversation.
- **Match the realtor's signoff style.** If past samples sign "— Holden" or "Holden | 616 Realty," use that. Don't invent a new signoff.

---

## How skills use this file

Every skill that produces written output for a realtor loads this file at start. After drafting, the skill silently checks: did I violate any tenet? If yes, redraft before showing the agent. If unsure, surface the specific concern to the agent and ask.
